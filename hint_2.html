  • Totally no idea about the starting?
    • First, you can set up a framework, which includes the given information and the final derivation.

    \[ \begin{array}{ll} 1.\big|&X \vee Y& ~\textup{given}\\ \hline 2.\big|&Y \rightarrow Z& ~\textup{given}\\ \hline &\\ &{...} &\\ \\ \hline ~~\big|&X \vee Z & \textup{...} \\ \end{array} \]


  • Get stucked after writing down the initial framework?
    • Read the OR rule, and try to apply it based on the framework you have.

    \[ \begin{array}{ll} 1.\big|&X \vee Y& ~\textup{given}\\ \hline 2.\big|&Y \rightarrow Z& ~\textup{given}\\ \hline &\\ &{\left[\mbox{$\begin{array}{rcl} 3a. \big|&X ~&\textup{...}\\ \hline &\textup{...} \\ \end{array}$ } \right]~~} {\left[ \mbox{$\begin{array}{rcl} 4a.\big|&Y ~&\textup{...} \\ \hline \textup{...} \\ \end{array}$} \right]\\} &\\ \\ \hline 5. \big|&X \vee Z & \textup{...} \\ \end{array} \]


  • Known that OR rule should be applied, but lost your goal when using OR rule?
    • After applying the OR rule, it's crucial to ensure that the outcome aligns with your ultimate objective, especially for less complex problems.
    However, in more intricate scenarios, the derivation of the OR rule may serve as an intermediate objective, acting as a bridge toward achieving the final goal.

    \[ \begin{array}{ll} 1.\big|&X \vee Y& ~\textup{given}\\ \hline 2.\big|&Y \rightarrow Z& ~\textup{given}\\ \hline &\\ &{\left[\mbox{$\begin{array}{rcl} 3a. \big|&X ~&\textup{...}\\ \hline ~~&\textup{...} \\ \hline ~~&X \vee Z&\textup{...} \\ \end{array}$ } \right]~~} {\left[ \mbox{$\begin{array}{rcl} 4a.\big|&Y ~&\textup{...} \\ \hline ~~&\textup{...} \\ \hline ~~&X \vee Z &\textup{...} \\ \end{array}$} \right]\\} &\\ \\ \hline 5. \big|&X \vee Z & \textup{...} \\ \end{array} \]
